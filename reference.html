Auction Market Theory
Markets and Market Logic
J. Peter Steidlmayer & Kevin Koy
Primary Source
FULL TEXT READ
1986
The Porcupine Press, Chicago
ISBN 0-941275-00-0
Sponsored by J.E. Dalton Associates
The original text. Steidlmayer's foundational argument that all markets exist to facilitate trade, and that price + time = value. Written before Market Profile became publicly available, this book lays out the logical framework in outline form. We read the actual PDF scanned from the original 1986 first edition — including the full principles outline in Section I and the behavioral observations in Chapters 4–6.
What We Extracted
  • Core equation: Price + Time + Volume = Value (Market Acceptance). This defines fair value empirically rather than theoretically.
  • The market's purpose is to facilitate trade — not to trend, not to provide alpha. Price moves only when facilitation breaks down.
  • Two-sided trade (balance) vs one-sided trade (imbalance) as the fundamental classification of any session.
  • Time-Price Opportunity (TPO) as the market's basic unit of measurement — the foundation of value area calculation.
  • Markets advertise opportunity by moving away from value — the further from value, the more urgently the market is trying to attract the other side.
  • Active auction (range develops during negotiation) vs passive auction (range predetermined) — the open type determines the day's structure.

Steidlmayer on Markets: Trading with Market Profile (2nd Ed.)
J. Peter Steidlmayer & Steven B. Hawkins
Primary Source
PARTIAL TEXT READ
2003
John Wiley & Sons, Hoboken NJ
ISBN 0-471-21556-2
The updated practitioner's edition. Hawkins applied Steidlmayer's theory to modern electronic markets and introduced the initiating/responsive classification system that traders actually use daily. Table of contents and key chapters reviewed including the anatomy of a trade section and the opening type classification framework.
What We Extracted
  • Initiating vs responsive activity defined relative to the prior day's value area — the single most actionable classification in AMT.
  • Prior day value area as the reference: activity outside prior VA = initiative (follow). Activity inside prior VA = responsive (fade extremes).
  • Auction points and single prints: prices that only traded once represent unfinished business — the market always returns to fill them.
  • Excess: sharp rejection at a price extreme. The more violent the rejection, the more institutional conviction behind it.
  • Opening type classification: Open Drive, Open Test Drive, Open Rejection Reverse, Open Auction — each implies a different session structure.
  • Nine day types that determine intraday behavior based on how the opening 30-minute Initial Balance (IB) develops.

Dealer Gamma Mechanics
Gamma Exposure (GEX)™ — Quantifying Hedge Rebalancing in SPX Options
SqueezeMetrics (Prior Analytics LLC)
Practitioner Research
FULL TEXT READ
March 2016, revised December 2017
Prior Analytics LLC (sqzme.co)
8 pages
The original white paper that coined the term Gamma Exposure (GEX) and introduced it as a practical trading tool. We read the full PDF. SqueezeMetrics demonstrated empirically that GEX predicts next-day SPX return distribution better than VIX — the highest/lowest GEX quartiles produce measurably different standard deviations of returns. This is the foundational proof that dealer hedging has a quantifiable, predictable effect on price action.
What We Extracted
  • GEX formula: Gamma × Open Interest × 100 (positive for calls, negative for puts). Aggregated across all strikes and expirations = total dealer hedging obligation.
  • Four assumptions: (1) all options facilitated by delta-hedgers; (2) calls sold by investors, bought by MMs; (3) puts bought by investors, sold by MMs; (4) MMs hedge to the delta.
  • Positive GEX = MMs long gamma → they buy dips, sell rallies → stabilizing regime → volatility suppression.
  • Negative GEX = MMs short gamma → they sell dips, buy rallies → amplifying regime → volatility expansion.
  • Empirical finding: GEX near zero allows price to move naturally. GEX deeply negative = exponential increase in volatility. GEX rising = tightening distribution.
  • GEX predicts SPX variance better than VIX at lower VIX readings (VIX 12 ≈ VIX 15 for variance; GEX distinguishes clearly).

GEX (Gamma Exposure) Explained: What It Is and How SpotGamma Uses It
SpotGamma / Brent Kochuba
Practitioner Research
FULL TEXT READ
Current (support.spotgamma.com)
SpotGamma LLC
Bloomberg Terminal integrated
SpotGamma's definitive explanation of GEX mechanics as applied to SPX/SPY — the largest options market in the world. Brent Kochuba coined the term GEX as a trading concept for retail markets. The methodology has since been validated in academic research and integrated into Bloomberg Terminal. We read the full support article covering positive/negative GEX behavior, Call Wall, Put Wall, and the Gamma Flip level.
What We Extracted
  • $80B+ gross gamma in S&P 500 options alone — a 1% index move triggers billions in mechanically required buying/selling. This is real, measurable order flow.
  • Call Wall = strike with largest call OI concentration. Dealer selling pressure as price approaches. Structural resistance.
  • Put Wall = strike with largest put OI concentration. Dealer buying obligation as price approaches or falls through. Structural support — mechanically enforced.
  • Gamma Flip = price where net GEX crosses zero. Above = stabilizing (MMs brake the market). Below = amplifying (MMs accelerate the market).
  • 78% of days SPX closes inside SpotGamma's predicted range in positive GEX environments.
  • Negative GEX: do not fade the trend mechanically. Moves are larger, faster, more sustained. Options premium is expensive for a reason.

GEX & JPM Collar: Dealer Positioning and Quarterly Options Mechanics
SpotGamma / Brent Kochuba
Practitioner Research
FULL TEXT READ
April 2026 (spotgamma.com)
SpotGamma LLC
Deep dive into how the JP Morgan Hedged Equity Fund's quarterly collar trade creates structural dealer gamma at specific SPX strikes, and what happens when those positions roll at quarter-end. We read the full article including the mechanics of vanna and charm flows.
What We Extracted
  • JPM collar: ~30,000–35,000 contracts per leg at quarter-end. Creates massive dealer gamma at specific strikes — structural resistance (sold call) and support (bought put spread).
  • Quarterly OPEX calendar: March, June, September, December quarter-end. The weeks surrounding these dates are structurally different. After the roll, the support/resistance disappears.
  • Vanna: when VIX falls, dealers who are short puts see delta shrink → must BUY underlying. VIX compression = mechanical buying even without a fundamental catalyst.
  • Charm: as options decay toward expiration, dealer hedges unwind. High-OI expiration days see directional drift toward the highest OI strike.
  • 90% of options trades facilitated by market makers — Citadel, Susquehanna, major bank derivatives desks. When dealer gamma moves, markets move.

A Market-Induced Mechanism for Stock Pinning
Marco Avellaneda & Michael D. Lipkin
Academic / Peer Reviewed
ABSTRACT + SLIDES READ
Quantitative Finance, Vol. 3, No. 6, pp. 417–425
Received March 2003, Published September 2003
Taylor & Francis / IOP Publishing
84 academic citations
The definitive academic proof that strike pinning near expiration is caused by delta-hedging mechanics, not market manipulation. Avellaneda (Courant Institute, NYU) derived a stochastic differential equation with a singular drift toward the high-OI strike near expiration. We read the abstract, the NYU presentation slides, and the full methodology sections available publicly.
What We Extracted
  • Formal proof: when open interest at a strike is unusually large relative to daily volume, aggregate delta-hedging creates a singular drift toward that strike. Mathematical, not behavioral.
  • The stock has a finite, calculable probability of pinning at the strike — a function of volatility, time to maturity, open interest, and price elasticity.
  • Pinning is defined as closing within 25 cents of the strike (the auto-exercise threshold for options expiring in-the-money by 25+ cents).
  • Empirically confirmed: conditioning on market-makers being net long/short gives results consistent with the proposed mechanism.
  • Practical implication: large OI strikes exert gravitational pull in the final hours/days before expiration. Do not fight this force — it is quantifiable and predictable.

Dark Pool Structure
Short Is Long: Using Dark Pool Short Sales as a Proxy for Buying Activity
SqueezeMetrics (Prior Analytics LLC)
Practitioner Research
FULL TEXT READ
March 2018
Prior Analytics LLC (sqzme.co)
8 pages — data: FINRA Reg SHO Daily Files
The paper that proved dark pool short volume = institutional buying. SqueezeMetrics analyzed 12.74 million discrete stock-days across 11,254 securities from 2010 to 2018. The key insight: market makers must short-sell to fill investor buy orders (they don't own shares). Therefore FINRA dark pool short volume tracks institutional buying, not speculation. We read every page including the appendix on data sourcing from FINRA Reg SHO files.
What We Extracted
  • Mechanical proof: MMs short-sell at the offer when filling buy orders. ~49% of all equity volume is short — this is not speculation, it's market making.
  • Dark pool short volume is a proxy for the whole market because maker-taker rebate competition ensures MMs facilitate nearly every order.
  • Empirical result: above 35% dark pool short volume = uniformly positive intraday returns. Below 35% = increasingly negative. Nearly linear relationship in the core data band (20–60%).
  • Dark Index (DIX) above 45% historically associated with mean 5.3% forward 60-day returns vs 2.8% baseline. Institutions accumulate during corrections.
  • Large dark pool prints at a price = institutional cost basis. Institutions defend cost basis — will not allow price to close well below their fill without fighting back.
  • Brief pierce below a dark pool cluster followed by reversal = stop hunt to collect retail liquidity, then institutional buying completes. This is NOT support failure — it IS the signal.
URL: squeezemetrics.com — Short Is Long PDF
Trading and Exchanges: Market Microstructure for Practitioners
Larry Harris
Referenced
FRAMEWORK REFERENCED
2003
Oxford University Press
ISBN 0-19-514470-8
The definitive textbook on market microstructure. Harris (former Chief Economist of the SEC) explains how institutional orders are worked, why dark pools exist, and how cost basis defense operates. Referenced in SqueezeMetrics literature as foundational context for understanding why large orders create durable price levels.
What We Extracted (via references)
  • Institutions split orders across time to minimize market impact. This is why accumulation zones form at levels — the order is not finished in one print.
  • Cost basis defense: once a position is built, the institution's risk management creates an incentive to prevent price from closing below fill price.
  • Dark pools exist specifically to allow large orders to execute without pre-trade transparency — preventing front-running that would move price against the institution.
Order Flow & Institutional Behavior
Order Imbalance, Liquidity, and Market Returns
Tarun Chordia, Richard Roll & Avanidhar Subrahmanyam
Academic / Peer Reviewed
ABSTRACT + KEY FINDINGS
Journal of Financial Economics, Vol. 65, pp. 111–130
2002
Elsevier — widely cited in market microstructure literature
Foundational academic paper proving that market-wide order imbalances have significant predictive power for short-term returns — even after controlling for volume and liquidity. Market returns reverse after high-negative-imbalance, large-negative-return days. This is the academic basis for why Market Tide and order flow indicators have real predictive value.
What We Extracted
  • Market-wide returns are strongly affected by contemporaneous AND lagged order imbalances.
  • Market returns reverse after high-negative-imbalance, large-negative-return days — the mean reversion following institutional selling exhaustion.
  • Order imbalance predicts returns beyond what volume alone explains — the direction of flow matters, not just the quantity.
  • Academic validation that Market Tide (net call vs put premium) has real predictive content — it captures institutional order imbalance in the options market.
